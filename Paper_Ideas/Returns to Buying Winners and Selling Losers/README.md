### This article "Returns to Buying Winners and Selling Losers: Implications for Stock Market Efficiency" of NARASIMHAN JEGADEESH and SHERIDAN TITMAN*, was published on THE JOURNAL OF FINANCE * VOL. XLVIII, NO. 1 * MARCH 1993.
link: https://www.bauer.uh.edu/rsusmel/phd/jegadeesh-titman93.pdf

### 1. Overview
The paper examines the predictability of stock returns based on past performance and establishes that stocks outperformed in the past tend to continue outperforming in near future. Otherwise, past losers tend to persist in underperforming ==> A phenomenon commonly referred to as "momentum".

### 2. Objectives
The authors aim to determine whether a trading strategy that systematically buys past winners and sells past losers can generate abnormal returns => Assess whether such predictability is consistent with the notion of market efficiency.

### 3. Methodology 
The study constructs a cross-sectional momentum strategy by ranking stocks according to their historical returns over a formation period, forming portfolios that longs the top-performing
stocks & short the worst-performing stocks => evaluating the performance of this long-short portfolio over a subsequent holding period to test for return continuation.

### 4. Key Findings
The results demonstrate evidences of return continuation over intermediate horizons, with momentum-based strategies yielding statistically significant abnormal returns that are robust across different samples,
suggesting that past performance contains information about future returns that is not fully captured by standard risk-based explanations at the time.

### 5. Interpretation
These findings challenge the strict interpretation of the "Efficient Market Hypothesis", as they imply that prices do not fully and immediately incorporate with available information, and instead adjust gradually in a way that allows predictable patterns to persist.

### 6. Limitations
Despite its influential results, the momentum effect is subject to episodes of sharp reversals and performance instability, 
is sensitive to trading costs and implementation frictions, and does not provide a complete theoretical explanation for why such predictability exists, 
leaving open questions about its underlying economic drivers.

### 7. Implications for Modern Finance
The paper has played a foundational role in shaping modern quantitative finance by establishing momentum as a core systematic factor used in asset pricing and portfolio construction, influencing the development of factor investing frameworks, algorithmic trading strategies, and behavioral finance theories that seek to explain market anomalies through investor psychology and information processing frictions.

### 8. Conclusion
Overall, the study represents a landmark contribution to empirical finance by providing compelling evidence of momentum in stock returns and demonstrating that simple trading rules based on past performance can yield abnormal returns, thereby challenging traditional views of market efficiency and forming the basis for a wide range of modern trading and asset allocation strategies.
